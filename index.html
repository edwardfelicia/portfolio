02 Case study · project rescue
Rescuing a stalled government IT project
Lead Project Manager · Scrum Master · ACPC PIMMS
The situation

I was brought onto a government procurement, inventory, and motorpool management system that was in trouble. Two project managers had already left, the build was roughly two months behind, and there was almost nothing to work from: no real documentation, no working process, and key client agreements existed only as verbal understandings. The team was capable but demoralized and working without a clear cadence.

What I did

I rebuilt how the project ran from the ground up. I introduced a full Scrum framework and taught the entire team how to use it, taking them from informal, ad-hoc standups to proper two-week sprints, daily standups, and structured reviews. I closed the documentation gap by making everything written and tracked, so work no longer lived in people's heads or in verbal promises. I re-established a regular client cadence with consistent demos and updates, which rebuilt trust after the earlier instability. And I controlled scope deliberately, honoring prior commitments in good faith while drawing a clear line on new requests so the timeline stopped slipping.

Before
  • Verbal, undocumented agreements
  • Sporadic, reactive client contact
  • Informal standups, no real process
  • Two months behind, two PMs gone
After
  • Everything written and tracked
  • Regular client demos and updates
  • Full Scrum: sprints, standups, reviews
  • Team trained and self-directing
The result

The system was delivered on time with a one-month buffer built in, full client sign-off, and no penalties. After the recovery, I was promoted to Lead Project Manager on the engagement.

03 How I operate
01
Write it down, or it didn't happen.
Knowledge that lives in someone's head is a risk. I make work visible, documented, and traceable so nothing depends on memory or verbal promises.
02
Build the system once, so it runs without me.
The goal isn't to be indispensable — it's the opposite. I design processes and documentation so teams keep moving whether or not I'm in the room.
03
Autonomy inside a clear structure.
People do their best work when they own their queue but know exactly how the pieces fit. I set the frame, then get out of the way.
04
Stop the bleeding, then fix the root cause.
For serious problems I triage first to contain the damage, then run structured analysis — impact mapping, Ishikawa, 5 Whys — to find what's actually broken. The goal is a sustainable fix, not a band-aid.
